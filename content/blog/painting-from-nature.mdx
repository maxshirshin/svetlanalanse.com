# Painting from Nature: Working with Live Specimens

There is no substitute for painting from a live specimen. Photographs can be useful references, but they flatten the subtle three-dimensional qualities that make botanical illustration so captivating.

## Why Live Specimens Matter

When you hold a flower in your hand, you can observe:

- The translucency of petals when light passes through them
- The way stems curve under the weight of blossoms
- Subtle colour gradations that cameras often miss
- The texture of leaves, from glossy to matte to velvety

## My Process

I typically begin a painting session by spending at least 30 minutes simply *observing* the specimen. I turn it in the light, note which angles are most interesting, and make small pencil sketches to plan the composition.

Only then do I begin mixing colours. For watercolour, I work from lightest to darkest, building up layers gradually. Each layer must dry completely before the next is applied — patience is essential.

## Tips for Beginners

1. **Start with simple forms** — A single leaf is an excellent first subject
2. **Work quickly when needed** — Some flowers will only last a few hours
3. **Keep the specimen fresh** — Place stems in water and mist petals lightly
4. **Take reference photos** — As backup, but always prioritise direct observation
